2,000-Step LLM Speedrun

Mixed English + Hindi, CPU-only, caps: ≤2,000 optimizer steps, ≤2,000,000 params, provided corpus only, pure PyTorch. Metric: bits per byte (bpb) on held-out text, lower is better.

Baseline bpb2.3718
Best bpb1.76
Improvement-0.61 (26%)
Params1.42Mof 2.0M cap

Best configuration

  • Tokenizer: byte-level BPE, vocab 1024 (256 raw bytes + 768 merges), trained only on train_corpus.txt. Lossless byte fallback, ~2.9 bytes/token on dev.
  • Architecture: GPT, 4 layers × 4 heads, d=160, block 128, weight-tied head/embedding, LayerNorm + GELU MLP. 1,421,760 params.
  • Optimizer: AdamW, betas (0.9, 0.95), weight decay 0.1 on 2D+ weights only, grad-clip 1.0.
  • Schedule: peak LR 1e-3, 100-step linear warmup, cosine decay to 0.1×.
  • Batch: 64 × block 128, full 2,000 steps.

Why this is the best metric under the caps

The binding constraint is the 2,000-step cap, not the parameter cap. Every gain came from learning more per step, not from a bigger model. We proved this directly: a run that spent nearly the full 2M-param budget (1.9M params, block 256, 5 layers) scored slightly worse (1.835) at 2.8× the wall-clock, because the larger model is undertrained at 2,000 steps. So we invested the budget in (1) a proper optimizer + LR schedule, (2) a corpus-trained BPE that packs ~2.9× more real context into the same block, and (3) a larger batch that feeds more tokens into each fixed step.

Key findings

  • Optimizer/schedule > everything free. Just fixing the constant LR gave the first big drop with zero extra params or compute.
  • Tokenizer is a per-byte multiplier. BPE shrinks Devanagari's 3-bytes-per-char penalty and multiplies effective context; bpb (per byte) improved even though per-token loss looks higher.
  • Tokens-per-step is the real lever. Steps are capped, so bigger batches (8→32→64) reliably help.
  • Bigger is not better here (negative result). Spending the full param budget undertrains the model at 2,000 steps and regresses. Reported deliberately.
  • Textbook init loses at short budgets. Plain N(0,0.05) beats GPT-2 scaled init because fast early learning matters more than depth-stable propagation over just 2,000 steps.

What I would try next (with more step/wall-clock budget)

  • Batch 96–128 with a re-tuned LR (~1.25e-3, shorter warmup). Batch was the dominant lever and was still improving at 64; I stopped only for CPU wall-clock. Most likely further win.
  • BPE vocab sweep (768 / 1280 / 1536). 1024 was a first guess; there's an unsearched sweet spot between sequence compression and embedding-parameter cost.
  • RMSNorm, bias-free linears, SwiGLU MLP — modern components that often help small models at equal params, tested one variable at a time.
  • Coverage-balanced sampling instead of random-with-replacement window starts, so all 2,000 steps see maximally diverse data.

Machine vs. human contribution

Human (the reasoning that scores): framed the step-cap (not param-cap) as the binding constraint; chose the experiment order and the one-change-at-a-time discipline; formed each hypothesis; decided to spend budget on optimizer/tokenizer/batch rather than model size; designed and ran the "max the parameter budget" experiment specifically to test and document the negative result; interpreted every outcome and decided what to keep or revert.

Machine (AI coding assistant): wrote the boilerplate — AdamW param-group split, warmup+cosine LR function, gradient clipping, CLI overrides in train.py; implemented the byte-level BPE trainer (train_bpe.py) and the lossless BPE tokenizer.py with byte fallback; ran the training/eval commands and transcribed numbers into RUNLOG.md. No architecture or hyperparameter decision was made by the model without a human hypothesis behind it.
